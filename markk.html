Markk - Designing at an early stage startup

Markk is an app that shows you what’s happening right now around you, or at a particular place. It’s a live rating app — like Yelp and Instagram Stories had a baby. Instead of lengthy text reviews, you share a photo or video from a place, and the ratings last for 24 hours only.

The app is gamified and users unlock Guru badges and earn points as they add ratings and engage with ratings created by other users.

Role

As a UX Engineer, I handle all kinds of design related tasks along with the senior designer. These include —

  1. UI and UX design
  2. Code for the web app
  3. Tracking user metrics

To get the above tasks done, I'm continuously collaborating with the developers and cofounders.

Tools & Tech Stack
  1. Figma
  2. Principle
  3. HTML, CSS, Javascript (Vuejs)
  4. CleverTap

Tagging Redesign
State of Tagging in July 2020

Markk has a unique “Phonebook Tagging” along with the usual internal users’ tagging. Users are able to privately tag their Phonebook contacts on their ratings. The contacts receive an SMS with a link to the rating.

But the feature was hardly being used, so I decided we needed to conduct user interviews to understand why this was happening and what needed to improve.

Finding users

I dug into CleverTap metrics and bucketed users into 4 groups —

  1. Users who had used Tagging
  2. Users who tapped on Tag but didn’t complete the process
  3. Users who rated frequently but never tagged
  4. Users who shared Markk ratings but never tagged (this was relevant because Phonebook Tagging is analogous to sharing your rating with a person who is not on Markk, they receive it as a text message.)

I then cherry-picked 21 of the most active users from the query results and emailed them about the interview along with Calendly links.

We finally managed to conduct 5 user interviews.

Conducting the Interviews

After some pre-testing questions to get context on the users and get them comfortable, we asked them perform the task of tagging a friend while adding a rating. We asked them to share their phone screen while doing so.

Profile Redesign
State of the Profile in June 2020

In June the COVID-19 lockdown was in full-swing in LA and we had few users venturing out, hence little content being created on Markk. We took the opportunity to work out some new features and simplify some existing features that could be shipped by the time users started moving out again.

New Requirements
All the requirements for this redesign came via the founders and were linked to the business goals and vision that they had for Markk.
We scrapped the multiple types of Badges we had on the app and created a new badge called the Guru badge.

By doing so, we narrowed down the ways to earn and grow on Markk to a more concrete and easily understood path.
These Gurus are our version of influencers. So we wanted to -
  1. Introduce the following and followers functionality along with them.
  2. Differentiate a Guru's profile from a regular user's profile.
Instead of the Live or Archived ratings, we wanted to the Going list (think Bookmarks) on the profile. Going and rating these bookmarked places is the quickest way of earning points.
We wanted the profile to look like something that the user could show off, with the number of Referrals being the currency of their influence on Markk. This was tied to the business model we are trying to implement.
Initial Sketches and Designs

The timeline was tight so I jotted down some thoughts and got straight to wireframing.

Something that I wanted to keep in mind while designing this screen was to try and show as much of the Live Ratings as I could above the first fold. For this reason, I was doing my best to lay out the profile details on top in as compact a way as possible.

I also toyed with the idea of surfacing the relevant users who had been viewing a certain Guru’s ratings or some relevant places that a certain Guru has rated before. (eg: “Has rated Malaka Spice, GroundUp and 3 others”)

To fix this issue, I created a design that was more along the lines of the existing design. It gave all the profile details more breathing space and a bright yellow background helped differentiate between Guru and non-Guru users easily.
